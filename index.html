Basic Info

Youngro Lee is a Postdoctoral Research Associate at Imperial College London working in machine learning and explainable AI (XAI) for biomedical data. His methodological focus is on turning high-dimensional, sparse, and longitudinal data into interpretable representations that are robust and clinically actionable. His current work centres on the longitudinal analysis and integration of clinical and molecular data (proteomics, methylation) in pulmonary hypertension. He has led over six interdisciplinary projects and authored nine peer-reviewed papers as first or co-first author, working across cardiology, pediatrics, microbiology, dentistry, and nephrology, and brings international research experience across Korea, Italy, and the UK.

Projects

Lead or co-lead role across all projects listed below.

Integrating longitudinal clinical and molecular data in pulmonary hypertension

Collaboration: Prof. Christopher Rhodes, Prof. Allan Lawrie (NHLI, Imperial College London); A*STAR (Singapore); University of Manchester

Integrating longitudinal clinical records with paired molecular measurements (proteomics and EPIC v2 methylation) across a deeply-phenotyped pulmonary hypertension cohort (~210 patients, four timepoints, ~10-year clinical follow-up), to characterise how molecular and clinical state co-evolve over the course of disease. The work develops interpretable representations that bring heterogeneous data modalities (clinical, methylation, proteomics) into a common, clinically-readable form, handling the differing scales, sparsity, and below-detection structure of each modality.

2026 ~

Modelling missingness and uncertainty in wearable-based CVD risk prediction

Collaboration: Dr. Juan Delgado San Martin, Dr. Haoting Zhang, Prof. Allan Lawrie (NHLI, Imperial College London)

Co-developing methods to characterise how wear-pattern missingness in multichannel wearable data affects confidence in cardiovascular-disease risk prediction, including missingness-mechanism analysis (MAR/MNAR), dynamic ensemble weighting across sensor channels, and partial temporal imputation.

2026 ~

Feasibility of smart watch-based blood pressure estimation

Collaboration: Prof. Hae-Young Lee, Department of Cardiology, Seoul National University Hospital; Samsung Electronics; Korean Hypertension Society

Watch-based blood pressure (BP) estimation holds great potential due to its convenience and low cost but faces challenges in accuracy and validation. Through nationwide campaigns organized by the Korean Hypertension Society, a large dataset of BP values measured by Galaxy Watch was collected (>1,700 volunteers). As lead for all computational analysis, Lee: 1) analysed statistical patterns within the data, 2) designed a validity index applicable to watch-based BP monitoring and identified factors affecting stability, and 3) developed a post-processing method to enhance accuracy efficiently.

2022 ~

Machine learning approach for biomarker discovery in microbial data

Collaboration: Prof. Barbara Di Camillo, Department of Information Engineering, University of Padova, Italy

Leveraging microbial data for disease biomarker identification, particularly in inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), introduces the challenge of dimensionality due to the vast range of phenotypes and limited sample sizes. To extract essential information from microbial data, two distinctive approaches were studied: 1) a Bray-Curtis similarity-based mapping transformation for feature selection, and 2) SHAP-based feature binarization to compress the abundance metrics of each taxonomy. Both approaches achieved more stable biomarker selection while maintaining or even improving the performance of the machine learning model.

2021 - 2026

AI assisted influenza like illness diagnostic system

Collaboration: Prof. Yun-Kyung Kim, Pediatric School of Korea University

Influenza-like illnesses pose significant public health challenges, resulting in substantial economic and societal impacts. To address these issues, a machine learning-based decision-support system was developed as a preliminary assessment tool for parents and doctors. Lee played a key role by developing the optimal machine learning model and validating its results using interpretability methods, and contributed to building a macro system for real-time data collection and prediction in a hospital setting.

2022 ~

Investigation on the validity of Explainable AI

Collaboration: Prof. Barbara Di Camillo, Department of Information Engineering, University of Padova, Italy; Georgia Institute of Technology

Feature importance is widely used to define risk indicators or biomarkers, yet its validity is often judged solely by model performance at the application level. Two distinctive studies were conducted: 1) analysing the relationship between model performance and the validity of feature importance, showing that data size — not model accuracy — drives stability of feature rankings; and 2) developing a Python library (CLE-SH) that summarises statistically valid analyses of SHAP values for clinician-friendly interpretation.
